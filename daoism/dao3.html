Chapter 3

Not exalting great men Prevents quarreling; Not parading treasures Prevents coveting; Not gloating of great thingsᵃ Prevents troubling;¹

Therefore the Sage leadsᵇ thus: Empties his heartᶜ,² Fills his gut,³ Weakens his ambitions,⁴ Strengthens his bonesᵈ;⁵ He does not tempt with knowledge And thus does not grow theirᵉ desires;⁶

Practice effortless action And all falls into place.⁷

¹ a theme between these is the idea of one's decisions causing envy, just envy in various forms; the first form is the envy for recognition, the section is envy for what another possesses, and the third is envy for what they cannot hope to possess; all this envy just causes issues, from quarrels, to thievery, to anxiety ² to be able to let go of desires ³ to be satisfied with what one has ⁴ to not strive too hard to force the outcome ⁵ to be strong in place where one needs to stand, firm and shaped by the ever changing currents that erode the unworked stone into something polished ⁶ one's choices affect others, bringing up envy, that desire to have what one doesn't; knowledge isn't what's bad, it's the weaponizing of specific knowledge causing others to lose their sense of contentment with their way that is ⁷ that is to say that when you don't force a specific outcome, when you don't grasp to a vision too tightly, things fall into order and harmonize

ᵃ this is actually an abstraction of what many translators often show as explicit acts such as "displaying what is desirable" or "showing rare treasures"; to me, these sound like it's about showing off or "gloating" about what one possesses ᵇ the term 治 (zhì) is often translated and read more bureaucratically, but I chose "to lead" to remove it from this and back to the idea of creating order from disorder; it also appears at the end of the chapter, showing that it's less about politics and domination and more about harmonization ᶜ the term 心 (xin) means "heart-mind" and is more often translated in this chapter as "mind" or something else related to "knowledge", making it sound more anti-intellectual than it really is ᵈ the term 骨 (gu), meaning "bones", was translated by Stephen Mitchell into "resolve", replacing its structural meaning with something more directional, losing its metaphoric capacity in favor of a distinct reading ᵉ in classical Chinese, the term 其 (qi) can be referring inwardly to the sage or outwardly to those the sage would lead; my decision to translate the first part of the section as "his" and the last one to "theirs" is that the conditions for order begin inward, not outward; a sage cannot hope to unburden others of that which he is burdened

Comments Overall, this chapter is rather straightforward: don't be a braggart, it doesn't help anyone and can cause more harm than not. Constructing social fields centered around comparison creates unnecessary tension.
